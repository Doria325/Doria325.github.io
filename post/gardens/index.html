Gardens: What's the Difference?

2024-07-17

“When we are surrounded by nature, a feeling of comfort comes over us, and our bodies become relaxed. Most of us would intuitively recognize this feeling, but until recently we haven’t had any evidence to prove it.” --Yoshigumi Miyazaki

Gardens have long been an expression of culture, philosophy, and artistry in numerous countries around the globe. In Asia, gardens are especially a representation of national spirit and cultural heritage. Several gardens are considered significant historic landscapes and architecture, part of the World Cultural Heritage. Chinese gardens and Japanese gardens are two popular types of gardens known for their charming and elegant designs that create a scaled-down world of nature.

Chinese and Japanese gardens are inspired by philosophy and religion.The use of water,plants, and rocks expresses the infinite meaning of nature in a symbolic way while allowing adventurers to release their stress and improve their concentration with the power of being surrounded by the pleasantness of nature. Both gardens demonstrate the concept of “borrowed scene,” which utilizes objects from far and near to create pleasing images from any spot on any angle of the garden and express continuity. The principles of aesthetics in the two gardens are to imitate nature and create miniature landscapes. Although both types of gardens share similar elements, some defining features and slight differences in details shape Japanese and Chinese gardens into two distinctive styles.

Japanese Gardens

Early Japanese gardens were inspired by Chinese gardens to a very large extent. Philosophical and spiritual elements, together with concepts of Buddhism and Taoism introduced from China to Japan in the 6th century, formed the initial aesthetic principles of Japanese gardens.

Architecture in Japanese gardens emphasizes minimalism, often uses very few colors, which are less vibrant, in a gray tone, and focuses on presenting the natural beauty of the structure and materials. The choice of materials focuses on simplicity, natural patterns, and texture. These choices are reflected on stone doors, suikinkutsu (water basins), and lanterns, which are regular elements that add harmony and balance to the garden. Japanese gardens may have chashitsu (tea houses), meditation halls, or sitting areas, but they are few in number and have a less diverse style of appearance. These buildings are mostly built with wood due to its abundance as a resource and also because of the country’s religion, Shinto, which views trees as divine; even after being cut into wood, they are still living beings.

Shot in Japanese Garden, Portland, during my summer break, 2024

Water bodies are exquisitely compacted, such as small streams and ponds, focusing on presenting the sense of flow and liveliness of water. Water flows with the wind or elevation throughout the garden. Streams, fountains, and water sounds create a calm vibe and represent renewal and continuity. This is an important element in both Japanese and Chinese gardens, but what I often find in Japanese gardens but not in Chinese gardens is the use of inconspicuous small streams that flow in corners throughout the garden. Japanese gardens do not emphasize the existence of water due to the geographic location of Japan as an archipelagic country and therefore have a greater focus on and fear of water. This can be seen in the use of sand and pebble stones in Karesansui, formed in the shapes of waves to represent water and islands. 

Shot in Japanese Garden, Portland

While designers pay great attention to the overall look of the landscape in Chinese gardens, more effort is spent on the details. Each placement of the object should be deliberately designed. For example, Japanese gardens often show their essence of beauty through rocks. In fact, the selection and placement of rocks and stones is a type of art that often contains specific rules and meanings. For instance, one important element, Karesansui, is a type of Japanese garden style and miniature landscaping consisting of fine sand and stones that are designed carefully in detail. It is a common scene in Japanese gardens that drives visitor to immersion throug tht art of stone placement. The detailing of Japanese gardens is the finishing touch, and the attention paid to the smallest things, such as a branch or mosses on a stone, is extremely important. The level of detail in garden construction and the positioning of objects are especially reflected in patterns formed from stones or inconspicuous illustrations curved on rocks.

Shot in Japanese Garden, Portland

Japanese gardens are very much inspired by Zen, a Japanese sect of Mahayana Buddhism. The belief of Zen is “elimination,” eliminating possessiveness, utilitarian thinking, and obsession with the past, and looking towards ourselves to reflect our real individuality. Inspired by Zen, the overall style of Japanese gardens emphasizes simplicity, serenity, elegance, and contemplation of natural landscapes.

Chinese Gardens

Chinese gardens are significant pieces of Chinese art and culture. The purpose of gardens changes from era to era, and designs also evolve throughout history. Starting in the Han dynasty, gardens were typically built in abodes for emperors to rest in the beauty of natural landscapes. These gardens eventually became popular tourist attractions. Today, we can still see traditional Chinese gardens in the homes of some residents.

One striking difference between the two types of gardens is that Chinese gardens typically have more buildings, such as different rooms to study, read, and entertain, and a diverse type of architecture, including pavilions, pagodas, gazebos, and small houses to break up sections in the garden, allowing each window and angle to show a distinct view from within. The Moon Gate is also a distinctive architectural feature of Chinese gardens, in which we cannot see any other types of gardens. Additionally, these architectures are usually more glamorous, with rich ornaments and colors. Unlike Japanese temples and tea houses that have straight and overhanging roofs, Chinese gardens tend to have eccentric, sloping rooflines. In general, structures in Chinese gardens are mostly round and use mostly curved lines. For example, the roofs of temples and pavilions show more sinuous variations of lines, like a dragon. From a distance, it looks like a blooming, vibrant flower, standing proudly with its head held high in the garden, as if everything around it is foil. On the other hand, buildings in Japanese gardens are mostly in quadrilateral form and use cleaner, straighter lines. Buildings tend to be shorter. Rather than compete for attention, they are more inclined to blend with the natural environment. Therefore, they do not have as powerful an aura as Chinese architecture, but they are exquisite and look more elegant.

Water bodies are mostly wide to emphasize vastness; therefore, most are lakes and larger ponds. Unlike Japanese gardens, Chinese gardens tend to make water bodies more attractive for visitors, such as short falls with rockeries instead of small streams. Lakes are often the central focus in Chinese gardens. These water forms usually come together with complex, exotic, and bold rockeries. Rockeries in Chinese gardens look very different from exquisite stones in Japanese gardens. They are sculptured as artificial mountains from individual stones in peculiar, porous shapes and symbolize a concentration of natural energy. Stones in Chinese gardens tend to imitate the forms of mountains as well as their imposing grandeur. This choice also reflects the Chinese pursuit of Shan Shui, which is translated to “mountain and water,” referring to landscape. 

Shot in the Humble Administrator's Garden, Suzhou, during my spring break, 2023

Similar to how Japanese gardens have strict rules in the placement of rocks, Chinese garden plants have clear meaning in particular locations for placement. For example, bamboos should be planted behind the house, osmanthus should grow in front of the hall, peonies in flower beds, parasols beside the staircase, and lilies and lotus should bloom in ponds. 

Shot in the Humble Administrator's Garden, Suzhou

Chinese gardens aim to express gloriousness. Designers consider how the placement of each object, far and close, creates beauty from any angle the viewer stands from. The composition of objects should imitate the appearance of falls and mountains in nature. Chinese gardens aim to create harmonious and aesthetic compositions from anywhere in the garden, therefore, designers pay great attention to the overall look and emotional expression of the landscape.

To Conclude

Garden is an individual’s interpretation towards nature. Due to differences in philosophy and religion, Chinese and Japanese gardens express varying emotions and meanings. The Chinese national aesthetics pursues the variety of architecture and livelihood, while Japanese aesthetics emphasizes wither and silence. Such differences shaped one to a captivating one and another to a simpler and exquisite one. As the garden designer Chen, Cong Zhou said, "Chinese gardens reveal nature within the artificial, while Japanese gardens reveal the artificial within nature." The highlights of Chinese gardens are how natural scenes, their essence, and their beauty are shown through the imitations of mountains and lakes. On the other hand, Japanese gardens convey the beauty of nature through the attention of artificial details and carefully planned designs using natural elements. Gardens, showing differences in how seemingly insignificant details are depicted creates dissimilar vibes, reflecting the uniqueness of nations and cultures.
